The Discography of Gorillaz

Gorillaz's discography is a testament to the band’s boundary-pushing creativity and its ability to adapt to the changing musical landscape. Since their debut in 2001, Gorillaz has consistently redefined what a band can be, blending a diverse array of genres, collaborating with artists from around the globe, and crafting immersive narratives through their music and visuals.

Each album represents a unique chapter in the band's journey, reflecting the creative vision of its creators, Damon Albarn and Jamie Hewlett. From the groundbreaking self-titled debut to the collaborative experiments of Song Machine, Gorillaz’s discography explores themes ranging from environmentalism and social issues to introspection and human connection.

Gorillaz

Gorillaz's self-titled debut album, released in 2001, introduced the world to the virtual band phenomenon. The album was a genre-defying blend of alternative rock, hip-hop, dub, and electronic music, showcasing the band's ability to merge diverse styles seamlessly. Produced by Dan "The Automator" Nakamura, the album featured standout tracks like “Clint Eastwood,” “19-2000,” and “Tomorrow Comes Today,” which became instant classics.

The album’s success was bolstered by its groundbreaking music videos, which brought the animated band members—2-D, Murdoc, Noodle, and Russel—to life. Gorillaz received widespread acclaim for its innovation and originality, earning the band its first Grammy nomination and paving the way for their future projects.

Demon Days

Released in 2005, Demon Days cemented Gorillaz's status as pioneers in modern music. This critically acclaimed album was produced by Danger Mouse and featured a darker, more introspective tone. Thematically, it tackled issues like environmental destruction, consumerism, and political unrest, blending these themes with haunting melodies and powerful collaborations.

Tracks like “Feel Good Inc.,” featuring De La Soul, “DARE,” with Shaun Ryder, and the epic “Dirty Harry” became iconic, earning the band several awards and global recognition. The album’s narrative-driven style and lush production made it one of the most influential albums of the 2000s, solidifying Gorillaz as a force to be reckoned with.

Plastic Beach

In 2010, Gorillaz released Plastic Beach, a concept album exploring themes of environmentalism, consumer culture, and the fragility of modern life. Featuring a wide range of collaborators, including Snoop Dogg, Lou Reed, Mos Def, and Bobby Womack, the album delivered a rich tapestry of sounds, from orchestral arrangements to hip-hop beats.

Standout tracks like “Stylo,” “On Melancholy Hill,” and “Rhinestone Eyes” showcased Gorillaz's ability to innovate while addressing urgent social issues. The album's visual world, centered on a fictional island made of ocean debris, further expanded the band's narrative universe. Plastic Beach was praised for its ambitious scope and remains a fan favorite.

The Fall

The Fall, released in 2011, marked a departure from Gorillaz's usual style. Recorded almost entirely on an iPad during the band’s North American tour, the album offers a more stripped-down, experimental sound. Damon Albarn described it as a sonic diary of life on the road, capturing fleeting moments and reflections in its music.

Songs like “Revolving Doors,” “Amarillo,” and “Phoner to Arizona” provide a more introspective glimpse into the band’s creative process. While not as commercially successful as its predecessors, The Fall received praise for its raw, experimental nature and stands as a testament to the band's willingness to take creative risks.

Humanz

After a six-year hiatus, Gorillaz returned in 2017 with Humanz, a vibrant, politically charged album reflecting on the tumultuous state of the world. Featuring an extensive lineup of collaborators, including Vince Staples, Grace Jones, Pusha T, and Mavis Staples, the album is a celebration of diversity and resilience in the face of chaos./p>

Tracks like “Saturnz Barz,” “We Got the Power,” and “Andromeda” showcase a more dance-oriented sound, blending pop, hip-hop, and electronic elements. Humanz was praised for its bold vision and the seamless integration of its eclectic features, marking a triumphant return for the band.

The Now Now

In 2018, Gorillaz released The Now Now, a more introspective and cohesive album. Unlike the feature-heavy Humanz, this project focused more on Damon Albarn's voice and songwriting, giving the album a more personal and reflective tone.

Songs like “Humility,” featuring George Benson, “Tranz,” and “Magic City” explore themes of solitude, change, and hope, set against a backdrop of funky, synth-driven production. The Now Now received critical acclaim for its focused approach, offering a more intimate look at the band’s evolving sound.

Song Machine, Season One

Song Machine, Season One: Strange Timez, released in 2020, is a unique album structured as a series of episodic releases. The album features an eclectic array of collaborators, including Robert Smith of The Cure, Beck, Elton John, St. Vincent, and EarthGang.

Tracks like “Aries,” “Strange Timez,” and “Momentary Bliss” highlight Gorillaz's unparalleled ability to adapt and experiment with new styles. The episodic nature of the project, accompanied by animated videos, brought a fresh approach to the album format, emphasizing collaboration and creativity during an unprecedented time for the world.

Cracker Island

Cracker Island, released in 2023, continues Gorillaz's tradition of innovation and storytelling. The album features prominent artists like Thundercat, Bad Bunny, Stevie Nicks, and Tame Impala, blending a mix of funky grooves, introspective lyrics, and upbeat anthems.

Tracks like the title track “Cracker Island,” “New Gold,” and “Silent Running” showcase the band’s continued evolution and their ability to remain relevant in an ever-changing musical landscape. Cracker Island was met with critical acclaim, proving that Gorillaz remains a trailblazer in modern music.
